Our Mission: Reconnecting Urbanization with Nature

Um Quarto 2.0 was developed based on the urgent need to reconnect urbanization with nature, recognizing that human well-being depends directly on the planet's health. Inspired by the words of Ailton Krenak, we reflect on the critical need to listen to and care for the Earth.

Our system aims to create this convergence, mapping how environmental factors—heat, pollution, and lack of green spaces—affect the mental balance of urban populations. The consequences of the climate crisis are already visible: 90% of the world's coastal cities face flood risk by 2050, where most of the global population lives.

With Um Quarto 2.0, we turn data into awareness—offering citizens, policymakers, and researchers a tool that reveals how mental health and the environment are deeply interconnected.

Climate Crisis and Mental Health: A Global Emergency

Air pollution affects more than 90% of the global population and is directly linked to the development and worsening of mental disorders, including anxiety, depression, and cognitive decline. Millions of premature deaths occur annually due to exposure to atmospheric pollutants, with severe neurobiological impacts on vulnerable populations.

90% of global coastal cities—where most of the world’s population lives—face flood risk by 2050. The climate crisis is not just environmental: it is an urban mental health crisis.

Neurobiological Mechanisms of Pollution Impacts

How Pollution Affects the Brain

Ultrafine particles of air pollution cross the blood-brain barrier, causing neuroinflammation and direct oxidative stress in brain tissue. This process results in changes in neurotransmitters, especially serotonin and dopamine, which are fundamental for mood and cognition regulation.

Measurable Impacts

  • ▸ 12-15% increase in anxiety and depression disorders with chronic exposure
  • ▸ 3-5% reduction in child cognitive development in polluted areas
  • ▸ Areas with less than 10% vegetation cover show 40% more psychological stress
  • ▸ Populations in dense areas without green spaces have 25% higher probability of mental disorders
  • ▸ Vegetation loss correlates with a 2-5°C increase in local temperature

Environmental Risk Factors

  • ▸ PM2.5 concentrations above 35 µg/m³ (dangerous levels)
  • ▸ Proximity to industrial zones and heavy traffic corridors
  • ▸ Absence of green spaces and cognitive recovery areas
  • ▸ High population density without adequate infrastructure
  • ▸ Urban heat islands that intensify physiological stress
  • ▸ Progressive loss of tree cover due to urban expansion

Integrated Analysis Methodology

Environmental Cognitive Load Index (ICCA) Model

The ICCA is calculated through a weighted model that integrates multiple sources of satellite data and environmental health indicators:

  • ▸ Air pollution (PM2.5, PM10, NO2, O3) - Weight: 30%
  • ▸ Vegetation loss and NDVI - Weight: 25%
  • ▸ Population density and human settlements - Weight: 20%
  • ▸ Surface temperature and heat islands - Weight: 15%
  • ▸ Proximity to stress sources (industry, traffic) - Weight: 10%

Satellite Data Processing

  • ▸ Multitemporal analysis of Sentinel-2 and Landsat 8 images for long-term trends
  • ▸ Change detection algorithms to identify forest loss and urban expansion
  • ▸ Atmospheric modeling to estimate pollutant concentrations
  • ▸ NDVI calculation for quantifying green areas and restorative potential
  • ▸ Infrared thermal analysis for mapping urban heat islands
  • ▸ Integration with demographic data to identify vulnerable populations

Validation & Application

The system is validated through epidemiological studies correlating environmental exposure with stress biomarkers, hospital records of mental disorders, and well-being perception surveys in urban communities. The results guide public policies for urban planning based on environmental neuroscience.
